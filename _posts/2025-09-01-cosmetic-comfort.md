Advertisements play a significant role in the daily lives of consumers,
despite being passive elements of the consumer’s surroundings for the
most part. While advertisements are perceived as purely promotional
material for a specific product, their effectiveness relies on taking
advantage of the consumers’ needs and emotions in order to entice
their attention. In “Branded For Life,” Andy Beckett reviews and presents
the argument that consumption is not intended to evoke a sense of
conformity for the consumer, but rather a sense of distinction.

He portrays the rise of counter-consumerist movements in the late 20th
century, citing as hippies and environmentalists, and argues that in
trying to stray from materialism they ended up creating smaller
consumerist ecosystems of their own (para. 11.) Critics such as Michel
Certeau disagree with the notion that consumers are prone to
advertising-based manipulation, arguing that they absorb and subvert
the imposed elements of media pressures for their own use and benefit.
In light of the critiques that can be made against Beckett’s methodology
and logic, he showcases a valid core argument that tackles a significant
sociological issue. Modern advertisements target the vulnerability of
individuals to persuade them into pursuing an artificial sense of
distinction (Beckett para. 7) which in itself is rooted on the foundations
of conformity, consumers then seek distinction from their social class
by attempting to conform to the attributes of another (Zygmunt, p. 7);
consequently, this phenomenon gradually shapes the values of society
while its extensive presence erodes consumer autonomy despite the
illusion of choice.

Consumers tend to believe that they make independent and unbiased
decisions, but they seldom possess a noticeable distinction from other
consumers and are instead prone to conformity. While consumers are
autonomous and self-aware in theory, the reality is that various external
factors influence the decisions they make and remove their autonomy
as a result; nonetheless, the consumer will still believe that they are
autonomous, leading to a perceived sense of autonomy that does not
reflect reality (Wertenboch.) Beckett argues that the consumer in a
modern capitalist society seeks distinction through materialism, and
does not in fact look for a sense of belonging or conformity.

It is true that the consumer desires uniqueness, but the author does
not consider the possibility for the consumer’s desire to be both 
unique and to conform. Conformity and distinction are relative concepts,
and if their subjects in relation are not the same, then the consumer
could in fact pursue both goals regardless of whether they are aware 
of it or not. Critics contend that the presence of a popular standard
like emotionally appealing advertising does not immediately prove that
consumers are prone to attraction or manipulation by it, but that the 
outcome of their reaction and use of this standard for their own benefit
determines the degree of their control over it (De Certeau, p. xviii.) 

Furthermore, the claim that the counter-cultural movements of the 1960s
contained consumerist elements which benefit capitalism (Beckett para. 7)
stands on the strawman argument that these movements promise a puritan 
separation from any acts of commerce or participation in the capitalist
market, and it also relies on the claim that rebelliousness has become 
the face of consumerism (Beckett para. 4.) Beckett errantly assumes the
belief that distinction and conformity are mutually exclusive constants
in order to substantiate his argument on the vulnerability of consumers
to advertising, which could’ve been more efficiently argued through more
objectively and empirical methods.

Advertisers use societal trends and sensitive personal information to distort 
or exaggerate reality to influence their audience, who are consequently 
convinced into thinking that the advertised product is necessary for their 
sense of distinction among their peers as well as for their sense of 
conformity among wider society (Beckett.) In the modern age of widespread 
technology and digital consumption, advertisements have become more 
personalised than ever towards each consumer and with this personalisation 
comes drawbacks and benefits. 

The consumer’s needs are met before they even realise it thanks to the extensive 
collection and analysis of the customer’s data and habits that makes advertisements
personalised. The means by which this data is collected has been criticised as 
invasive, disregarding of the consumer’s rights to privacy and autonomy, as 
well as exploitative of the consumer’s sensitive information to sell them the 
right product at the right time (André et al. 5.) Anti-consumerist subcultures 
and trends have been incorporated into mainstream advertising (Beckett para. 3-4,) 
which goes to show that even dissent can be commodified and the consumer does not 
have any real freedom of choice. Critics dismiss this argument, arguing that the 
amount of personalisation in advertising does not correlate to the degree of consumer
autonomy present (Holmen.)

The values of consumers are subject to change, particularly through the influence of external 
media such as advertisements; consequently, advertisements become a convenient yet subtle 
tool for the gradual manipulation of societal values as seen fit by those disseminating 
them. It can be argued that today, the most successful manner of selling the greatest 
amount of any product is to convince the consumers that they need it to obtain an 
artificial sense of distinction (Beckett para. 6,) and the means used to achieve 
this level of commercial success came with significant social and ethical consequences.
These ethical repercussions are evident as seen for example in the widespread body image
issues among a significant number of Americans, with the average perception of the 
desirable body type among them being similar if not identical to the ideal bodily 
standard promoted by advertisers (Sustainability Directory.) 

The manner in which advertisements are used by the beauty industry subtly targets common points 
of insecurity among consumers and exacerbates these insecurities (Velasquez, p. 15) to evoke 
the consumer’s interest for the promoted products further; furthermore, the consumers are 
subconsciously impacted by this persuasive rhetoric, leading to a shift in their values 
and ultimately a noticeable gradual impact on societal values or behaviour (Beckett para. 6.)
While critics may argue that the factors of consumer susceptibility are not as significant as
they are portrayed to be (Coppock et al.,) evidence overwhelmingly shows that companies have 
long used persuasive and emotive elements to implant values and perceptions that increase the 
desirability of their products to the audience. Corporations disregard the moral consequences 
that derive from the persuasive rhetoric they employ in advertising, effectively stripping the
consumers from having an informed volition by feeding them a distorted reality to gain profit.

Consumers are presented with the illusion of choice, and it is through their ignorance of their 
subjection to control that they are made vulnerable to other external influences disseminated by
contemporary marketing. Advertising is not a reflection of existing societal norms and values, 
but rather a distortion of them in the manner most beneficial to private entities that stand to
gain profit from this distortion of values. The pervasiveness and influence of advertising is 
shown through the infiltration, distortion and commercialisation of rebellious subcultures by 
corporate sponsors during the late 20th century, although critics argue that this detracts from
the uncomfortable truth that this commercialisation was imminent without the existence of these
companies to begin with (Beckett para. 7.) 

It is important to note, however, that rebellious subcultures that continue to exist without 
adulteration or commercialisation, as noted by Beckett through the example of pacifists which
have never been impacted by the commodification of dissent (para. 11.) Companies don’t just 
rely on faux rebellion, though, as advertisements that promote fitness supplements, equipment,
or services such as gym memberships are one of the most common and effective types of promotional
material today. The appeal of fitness advertisements is drawn not from making the product or 
service itself directly seem more desirable, but through invoking emotions of self-consciousness,
inferiority, or contention in the audience by using exceptionally untroubled, attractive models 
that are seen promoting and giving positive testimony for these products or services which leads
to the viewers internalising these external values of beauty (Velasquez, p. 17.) 

Corporations thrive on the deception of consumers into a sense of security, so that they may drive
them towards a vulnerable condition where it is easy to embed specific values on them and convince
them that a certain product is the key to distinction among their peers (Beckett para 8.) While 
this endeavour has historically been successful in keeping consumers attached to superficial 
products and services that they do not need out of being subject to subliminal influence in 
advertising, this profit comes at the cost of distorting societal values which may lead to 
significant consequences over time.

Advertisements are perceived as passive and unimportant features of everyday life, only being a nuisance 
at worst but nothing inherently significant or concerning. Evidence shows that this cannot be further 
from the truth, and that their pervasiveness and influence targets the vulnerability of individuals 
to persuade them into pursuing an artificial sense of distinction which in itself is rooted in conformity.
Consumers who subconsciously seek distinction from their social class by paradoxically attempting to
conform to the attributes of another are especially susceptible to the subtle cues and persuasive 
elements embedded in promotional materials. This psychological dynamic extends far beyond mere 
persuasion, it shapes societal values through the emotions it invokes and gradually erodes consumer
autonomy through the data it collects, all while maintaining the illusion of choice.
